The sea is rising quietly. Atoll nations are running out of room to retreat.

Not a projection — a record

The figures on this page aren't a model of the future. They are annual anomalies that have already happened, recorded since 1993 and drawn from official Pacific Community (SPC) indicators for the region.

Centimeters, not abstractions

Many atolls across Kiribati, Tuvalu, and the Marshall Islands sit only about one to two meters above sea level on average. At that elevation, ten centimeters is not a small number.

Uneven, but never reversed

Currents, winds, and climate oscillations like El Niño–La Niña make the pace of rise differ from island to island. But across all 21 monitored territories, the long-term direction is the same: up.

01 / Context

Faster than the planet's average.

Global sea levels have risen at roughly 3.3 mm per year on average since the start of the satellite altimetry era in the early 1990s — itself already a historically fast rate compared to the 20th century.

This Pacific basin is rising faster still. The 31-year trend in this dataset works out to about 4.3 mm/year — roughly a third again above the global mean. The bars to the right put both rates on the same scale.

Global figure is a widely cited satellite-era average (NOAA / IPCC AR6 order of magnitude), shown for scale — not a precise reconciliation of differing source methodologies. Pacific figure is computed directly from this dataset's basin mean via linear regression.

03 / Trend

A line that isn't flat anymore.

The basin-wide annual average fluctuates year to year, but its five-year trend has moved in one direction since the mid-2000s. Shaded bands mark strong El Niño and La Niña episodes — the natural climate swings that help explain the year-to-year noise.

Reading the bumps: the sharp dip in 1997–98 and the swing around 2015–16 line up with two of the strongest El Niño events in the satellite record, while 2010–11 coincides with a strong La Niña. These oscillations shift water around the Pacific from year to year — but they sit on top of the rising trend, not in place of it.

07 / Findings

What this data shows.

FINDING 01

Not one has gone down.

Of the 21 Pacific countries and territories monitored since 1993, every one recorded a net rise by 2023 — ranging from +8 cm to +18 cm. None sits below its own baseline.

FINDING 02

Year-to-year noise hides, but doesn't erase, the long-term direction.

The basin average moved from roughly 0 cm in 1993 to +10.5 cm in 2023, at a pace of about 4.3 mm/year — faster than the global ocean average of roughly 3.3 mm/year. Annual spikes and dips still appear, often tracking El Niño–La Niña cycles, but the five-year trend has climbed consistently since the mid-2000s.

FINDING 03

The fastest pace isn't always where the spotlight is.

Palau, Micronesia (FSM), Guam, Papua New Guinea, and the Solomon Islands all recorded rises of ≥14 cm since 1993 — matching or exceeding Tuvalu and Kiribati, the islands most often cited as symbols of this crisis.

Data quality note

Why some early years read as exactly zero

SPC's source values are rounded to the nearest 0.1 m before publication — a coarse resolution relative to the true signal. Several countries show a string of "0" anomalies through the 1990s; this likely reflects that rounding floor rather than the sea genuinely standing still. Treat single-year, single-country readings as indicative rather than precise, and lean on the multi-year smoothed trend and the basin-wide average for the more reliable signal.
